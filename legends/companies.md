# CoCo Companies & Publishers

Organizational view of the CoCo ecosystem — the companies, publishers, and outfits that built the platform's commercial identity, and the people behind them.

This is a working research document. Many entries need primary-source verification. Uncertain claims are marked **(verify)**.

## Hardware Manufacturers

**Tandy / Radio Shack** — original manufacturer. CoCo 1 (1980), CoCo 2 (1983), CoCo 3 (1986), MC-10 (1983). Fort Worth, TX headquarters. The platform originated in a 1977 joint venture between Tandy and Motorola Semiconductor (Austin, TX), initially targeting an experimental videotex terminal project for the Kentucky Cooperative Extension Service / University of Kentucky College of Agriculture. Key Tandy personnel: **Mark Siegel** ("father of the CoCo 3"; chief internal champion), **J.M. Prickett** (Tandy co-designer of the GIME chip), **Ed Juge** (executive / public spokesman for the CoCo line), **Steve Bjork** (contracted CoCo 3 developer; advocated for enhanced IRQ timer functionality), and **Robert Arnstein** (in-house programmer for cassette/disk titles).

**Microsoft** — wrote the BASIC ROMs under contract to Tandy. **Bill Gates** is publicly credited by Microsoft and Wikipedia as the designer of Color BASIC, and stated in period interviews that he wrote Extended Color BASIC himself. Specific supporting Microsoft engineers are not well documented. Versions: Color BASIC 1.0 (1980, 4K), Color BASIC 1.3 (1982), Extended Color BASIC, Disk Extended Color BASIC.

**VLSI Technology, Inc.** — fabricated the GIME ASIC for the CoCo 3. **J.L. Bruister** (VLSI) is the named co-designer alongside J.M. Prickett (Tandy) on the chip's circuitry.

**Microware Systems Corporation** — founded 1977 in Clive, Iowa (Des Moines suburb) by **Ken Kaplan** and **Larry Crane**, with early engineer **Robert Doggett**. Began under an NSF grant developing software for the Motorola 6800. After Motorola released the 6809 in 1978, Microware shifted focus and produced **BASIC09**, then built the first version of **OS-9** around it. OS-9 was licensed to Tandy as **OS-9 Level I** (CoCo 1/2) and **OS-9 Level II** (CoCo 3). Microware also wrote the **Super Extended Color BASIC** ROM extensions for the CoCo 3 (1986) — the augmentations that gave the platform 80-column text and enhanced graphics/palette commands. **Boisy Pitre** joined Microware as a software engineer in 1992. Ken Kaplan retired in 2002 after 25 years. RadiSys Corp. acquired Microware in September 2001; rights to OS-9 were later purchased by a distributor group on March 1, 2013, forming **Microware LP**. (Note: Terry Ritter — often miscited as a Microware co-founder — was actually at Motorola, designed the 6809, and published foundational OS-9 modular-design articles in *Byte* Jan–Mar 1979. His influence shaped OS-9 but he was not a Microware principal.)

**Frank Hogg Labs (FHL)** — **Frank Hogg**. Longtime OS-9 advocate and distributor. Hardware line: *TC-9 "Tomcat"* (6809-based using the CoCo 3's GIME chip for compatibility; 1MB RAM, 2 serial + 1 parallel port, serial mouse, 8-bit DAC/ADC), *TC-70* (68000-class K-Bus system similar to MM/1, stand-alone or TC-9 companion, expandable to 14+ MB), and a catalog of **K-Bus** expansion cards (K-CPU-030, K-DRAM-2M, K-SCSI, K-FDC, K-ACI, K-PAR).

**Cloud-9 Tech** — **Mark Marlette**. CoCo 3 hardware expansions, including the SuperBoard and SuperIDE. The most visible modern-era hardware house for the platform.

**CRC Computers Inc.** (CRC Ordinateurs Inc. / **Disto Super Products**) — **Tony Di Stefano**, owner. Established 1983 in Montreal, Quebec, Canada. Comprehensive Disto product line: *Super Disk Controller* (original), *Super Disk Controller II* (sold in Radio Shack stores), *Mini Controller*, *Mini EPROM Programmer*, *DISPLAY80* (80×24 display card with real-time clock and parallel printer interface), *Hard Disk II Adapter*, *Super RAMDisk* (512K, upgradable to 1MB), *Super RAM 3* (512K with OS-9 Level 2 utilities), *MEB Adapter* (mini-expansion-bus), *RS-232 SuperPack*. The Super Controller uses the Western Digital WD1773 floppy controller chip. One of the biggest third-party hardware names of the 1980s CoCo era.

**Hawksoft** — **Christopher R. (Chris) Hawks**. Based in Harvard, Illinois. Long-running CoCo hardware and software vendor. Products include *CDF OS9* (NitrOS-9 driver for reading PC CD-ROMs), *RGB-to-S-Video Converter* (eliminates need for the specific CoCo 3 RGB monitor), and the *Raspberry Pi CoCo* project (Pi-based CoCo 3 lookalike running emulation). Contact: chawks@dls.net.

**CoCoSDC** — **Ed Snider**. Modern SD card interface for real CoCo hardware. Became the de facto storage solution for contemporary CoCo users.

**J&M Systems, Ltd.** — disk controllers and drive systems, based in New Mexico. Three controller models: *JFD-COCO* (original, gold contacts, JDOS 1.11 ROM, 6ms stepping — not CoCo 3 compatible), *JFD-CP* (built-in Centronics parallel port, compatible with 5MB Seagate hard drive system, "most deluxe"), *JFD-EC* (economy model, CoCo 3 compatible, dual ROM capability). Widely regarded as among the best CoCo disk controllers. **Alan Stallings** is documented as formerly of J&M; founder's name not yet confirmed. Related: **Owl-Ware** (companion imprint).

## Game Publishers

**Adventure International** — Founded by **Scott Adams** in 1979, Longwood, Florida. One of the earliest software publishers in the home-computer era and the dominant text-adventure publisher of the early 1980s. CoCo catalog included the full *Scott Adams Adventures* series (*Adventureland*, *Pirate Adventure*, *Mission Impossible*, *Voodoo Castle*, *The Count*, *Strange Odyssey*, *Mystery Fun House*, *Pyramid of Doom*, *Ghost Town*, *Savage Island* I & II, *Golden Voyage*, *Sorcerer of Claymorgue Castle*) and the Marvel-licensed *Questprobe* adventures (*The Hulk*, *Spider-Man*). Also published action and arcade titles by other authors. Ceased operations in 1985 following financial difficulties. **(verify CoCo-specific catalog breadth and AI's non-Adams CoCo titles.)**

**Spectral Associates** — one of the most prolific CoCo game houses. Co-founded by **Ken Kalish**. Key developers included **Steve Bjork** and **Steve Ostrom**. Titles included *Zaxxon*, *Astro Blast*, and numerous Tandy-licensed products.

**Tom Mix Software** — prolific CoCo game publisher. *Doubleback*, *Katerpillar Attack*, and many others. Instantly recognizable packaging and one of the iconic CoCo game imprints.

## Application & Productivity Software Publishers

**Cognitec** — **Howard Cohen**. *Telewriter* (November 1983), *Telewriter-64*, *Telewriter-128*. The first "world-class" CoCo word processor and the dominant pre-OS-9 word processor on the platform.

**Softlaw Corporation** (formerly **Nelson Software**, renamed 1983) — *VIP Writer*, *VIP Calc*, *VIP Database*, *VIP Terminal*, *VIP Integrated Library*. The most prominent integrated productivity suite for the CoCo. Principal programmer not yet verified; company founder's surname was Nelson. Source code rights to VIP Writer III later transferred to **SD Enterprises**.

**Spectro Systems** — **Art Flexser**. *ADOS*, *ADOS 3*, *Extended ADOS 3*. The most popular modified RS-DOS for the CoCo — added 40/80-track drive support, double-speed disk I/O, command-line editing, auto line numbering, and macros.

**Eigen Systems** — **Mark Davidsaver**. *Colorcom/E* terminal program with cassette file transfer, auto data capture, off-line print-outs, and selectable RS-232 options.

**PXE Computing** — *AutoTerm* (6.3D). CoCo terminal program. **(verify individual author)**

**Bob van der Poel Software** — **Bob van der Poel**. Large catalog across OS-9 and Disk BASIC: *Ved* (8K 6809 assembler text editor), *Vprint* (text formatter), *Ultra Editor*, *Ultra Label Maker*, *Stock Manager*, *RMA Assembler Library*, *Basic09 Subroutine Pack*, *CoCo 3 Cribbage* (1987), *Our DOS*, *Disk Utility Pack II*, *Magazine Index System*, and more.

**Dale Rickert** (independent) — *Simply Better* word processor for CoCo 3. A respected CoCo 3-era word processor with a substantial (~137-page) manual.

**Tandy / Radio Shack** — **Robert Kilgus** wrote *Color SCRIPSIT* (1981, cat. 26-3105, cartridge), expanded as a disk version in 1982 (26-3255). *Color SCRIPSIT II* (1987, 26-3109) was an entirely new program, not a revision. Also *DeskMate* and the broad first-party productivity line.

## OS-9 Ecosystem Vendors

**Microware Systems** — see Hardware Manufacturers; the root of the OS-9 ecosystem.

**Burke & Burke** — **Chris Burke**, principal. Late-1980s / early-1990s CoCo 3 hardware and software vendor. Flagship product: **CoCo XT Hard Disk Interface** (v2.4 1989, v2.5 1990) — enabled CoCo connection to IBM-compatible MFM/RLL controllers for ST-506/412 hard drives; bundled the CoCo XT software, a Western Digital MFM controller, cables, and documentation. **CoCo XT-RTC** added real-time clock functionality. Additional OS-9 utilities and (widely cited in period Rainbow ads) a product line including *PowerBoost* — though PowerBoost details need verification from primary sources.

**Hyper-I/O** — **Stephen Goldberg**. *HyperDriver* hard drive system and drivers for OS-9 on CoCo. **(Details still pending; web search returned no substantial period sources — primary documentation needed.)**

**Frank Hogg Labs** — see Hardware Manufacturers; also distributed OS-9 software and documentation.

**Sub-Etha Software** — **Allen Huffman**. OS-9-era utilities and publications; ongoing CoCo blog and historical writing.

**The NitrOS-9 Project** — community-maintained modernized OS-9 for the CoCo. Originally created by three Canadians — **Bill Noble**, **Wes Gayle**, and **L. Curtis Boyle** — as an enhanced OS-9 Level II for the CoCo 3. From version 3.2.8 onward, the project grew a substantial modern contributor base including **Boisy Pitre**, **David Ladd**, **William Astle**, **Robert Gault**, and **Aaron Wolfe** — adding DriveWire support, SuperDriver, hardware ports, and many driver fixes. Hosted at nitros9.org and at [github.com/nitros9project/nitros9](https://github.com/nitros9project/nitros9). Companion distribution: **NitrOS-9 Ease of Use (EOU)** — maintained by L. Curtis Boyle as the practical user-facing build.

## Magazines & Press

**Falsoft, Inc. / The Rainbow Magazine** — Prospect, Kentucky. *The Rainbow* (July 1981 – May 1993) was the connective tissue of the CoCo community. Also produced *Rainbow on Tape* and *Rainbow on Disk* companion media, and published CoCo books under the imprint (see Dale Puckett and Peter Dibble below).

Masthead (compiled across 1985–1992 issues):
- **Lawrence C. "Lonnie" Falk** — Editor and Publisher throughout the magazine's run. Wrote the monthly editorial column **"PRINT#-2,"** (the trailing comma is deliberate — it's CoCo BASIC syntax for printing to the printer).
- **James E. "Jim" Reed** — Managing Editor (1985–1987), later promoted to Assistant Vice President of Programs & Projects at Falsoft.
- **Cray Augsburg** — Technical Editor (1987), later Managing Editor (1992) — took over the top editorial role for the magazine's final years.
- **Courtney Noe** — Senior Editor (1985).
- **Dan Downard** — Technical Editor (1985).
- **Dale L. Puckett** — author of the *KISSable OS-9* column (premiered October 1983), the platform's definitive recurring OS-9 column.

Contributing Editors (1985 masthead): Bob Albrecht, R. Bartly Betts, Steve Blyn, R. Wayne Day, **Tony DiStefano** (yes, the Disto founder), Dennis Kitsz, Joseph Kolar, Michael Plog, Dale Puckett, Fred Scerbo, Richard White.

Notable Falsoft book imprint publications:
- **Dale L. Puckett & Peter Dibble**, *The Complete Rainbow Guide to OS-9* (1985) — 400+ pages plus two accompanying disks; the canonical CoCo OS-9 reference.
- **Dale L. Puckett**, *Microware's Official Basic09 Tour Guide*.

**Hot CoCo** — competing CoCo magazine from Wayne Green Publishing. **(verify dates and key contributors)**

**Color Computer Magazine** / other early periodicals — **(verify)**

**FARNA Systems** — **Frank G. Swygert**. Publisher of *Tandy's Little Wonder: The Color Computer 1979-1991* (1993) — the canonical printed reference on the platform's history, hardware, software, and community.

## Modern Tools & Cross-Development

**Lost Wizard Enterprises / lwtools** — **William Astle** (Canada). *lwasm* / *lwlink* — the modern 6809/6309 cross-assembler and linker toolchain; the de facto standard for contemporary CoCo development. lwasm is the assembler used to build the NitrOS-9 project itself (kernel, file managers, device drivers, utilities), so it is extensively battle-tested. Astle's CoCo projects and writings live at lost.l-w.ca.

**CMOC** — **Pierre Sarrazin**. Modern C compiler targeting 6809/CoCo.

**XRoar** — **Ciaran Anscomb**. Accurate CoCo/Dragon emulator and the primary cross-dev test environment in current use.

**MAME CoCo driver** — original driver by **Nathan Woods** **(verify)**; current maintenance and accuracy work led by **Tim Lindner**.

**Toolshed** — cross-development utilities (decb, os9, etc.). **(verify maintainers)**

**DriveWire 4** — PC-as-disk-server over serial. **(verify authors — candidates include Aaron Wolfe, Jim Hathaway, Bob Devries)**

**GCC 6809** — modern C toolchain port. **(verify — Brett Gordon and others)**

## Community Organizations

**Glenside Color Computer Club (GCCC), Inc.** — Carpentersville, Illinois (Chicago area). "The Oldest and Best Computer Group for the TANDY Color Computer." Hosts the annual **Chicago CoCoFEST!** (the 34th edition is scheduled for April 24–25, 2026). Publishes the *CoCo~123* newsletter/magazine (running since at least 1998, ongoing).

Current leadership:
- **Jim Brain** — President (also well-known in wider retrocomputing as founder of Retro Innovations).
- **John Mark Mobley** — Vice President.
- **Bob Swoger** — Communications Director.
- **Grant Leighty** — CoCoFEST Coordinator.
- **Ian Mavric** — CoCoFEST co-organizer.
- **Tony Podraza** — past President; CoCoFEST Coordinator Emeritus. Historically handled much of the event's detail work (with Bob Swoger) for decades.

**The CoCo Crew Podcast** — long-running modern-era community podcast (99+ episodes as of Dec 2024). Hosted by **John W. Linville** and **Neil** (Neil's full name needs verification). Regular voices in the extended lineup include **Mike** and **Boisy Pitre** (boisy@cococrew.org). Available on Spotify, Apple Podcasts, YouTube, and cococrew.org.

**CoCoTALK! / The CoCo Nation Show** — weekly live, interactive talk show broadcast Saturdays at 2pm Eastern on YouTube. Tagline: "The World's First Live and Interactive Talk Show featuring the Tandy Color Computer and its Hardware Cousins." 465+ episodes, 109,000+ downloads. **L. Curtis Boyle** is a regular contributor with recurring "news" segments. Frequent featured guest: **John Strong** (Strongware). (Primary host name needs verification — the show was originally CoCoTALK!, rebranded to The CoCo Nation Show.)

**The CoCo List** (mailing list) — primary community discussion venue for decades.
