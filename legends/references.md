# CoCo Hall of Fame — References & Sources

Primary and secondary sources behind the attributions in [README.md](README.md), [companies.md](companies.md), and [whos-who.md](whos-who.md).

Every non-obvious claim in the Hall of Fame should trace back to an entry here. When a new source is used, add it. When a claim is superseded or corrected, leave a note on what changed and why.

This document is organized by source type: books, archives, community histories, interviews, personal sites, and other primary sources.

## Books

### *Tandy's Little Wonder: The Color Computer 1979-1991*
- **Author:** Frank G. Swygert (F.G. Swygert)
- **Publisher:** FARNA Systems, 1993 (second printing; first edition April 1993)
- **Access:** [Color Computer Archive (PDF)](https://colorcomputerarchive.com/repo/Documents/Books/Tandy's%20Little%20Wonder%20(1993)(Farna%20Systems).pdf), [Internet Archive (OCR text)](https://archive.org/stream/Tandys_Little_Wonder_1993_Farna_Systems/Tandys_Little_Wonder_1993_Farna_Systems_djvu.txt)
- **Used for:** Nelson Software → Softlaw rename (1983); SD Enterprises acquiring VIP Writer III source rights; Art Flexser / ADOS; Mark Davidsaver / Colorcom-E; H. Allen Curtiss / desktop publishing; overall commercial CoCo history framing.

### *Color Computer Assembly Language Programming* — William Barden Jr.
- **Publisher:** Radio Shack
- **Used for:** Barden's role as foundational CoCo educator (pending deeper citation with edition dates).

### *CoCo: The Colorful History of Tandy's Underdog Computer*
- **Authors:** Boisy G. Pitre and Bill Loguidice
- **Publisher:** CRC Press, 2013 (ISBN 978-1466592476)
- **Access:** [Routledge / CRC Press](https://www.routledge.com/CoCo-The-Colorful-History-of-Tandys-Underdog-Computer/Pitre-Loguidice/p/book/9781466592476), [Google Books preview](https://books.google.com/books?id=k5bNBQAAQBAJ)
- **Used for:** GIME designer attribution (J.M. Prickett / Tandy + J.L. Bruister / VLSI Technology); first-hand accounts from Tandy executives and engineers; Mark Siegel as "father of the CoCo 3"; the Tandy–Motorola Semiconductor joint-venture origin story; general CoCo commercial history framing.

### *The Complete Rainbow Guide to OS-9*
- **Authors:** Dale L. Puckett and Peter Dibble
- **Publisher:** Falsoft, Inc., 1985 (ISBN 978-0932471000)
- **Scope:** 400+ pages plus two accompanying disks — the canonical CoCo OS-9 reference of its era.
- **Used for:** Confirming Puckett/Dibble co-authorship; OS-9 Users Group leadership (Puckett President, Dibble VP).

### *Microware's Official Basic09 Tour Guide* — Dale L. Puckett
- **Used for:** Puckett's authorship bibliography beyond the KISSable OS-9 column.

## Attribution Corrections

A running log of mistakes caught during research, so future readers understand why entries changed.

- **Simply Better** — earlier drafts attributed this to Bob van der Poel. Correct author: **Dale Rickert** (confirmed via comp.sys.tandy thread, 2000). van der Poel's actual catalog is extensive but does not include Simply Better.
- **VIP Software / Softlaw** — earlier drafts listed "VIP Software / Softlaw" as one entity. Actual history: the company was originally **Nelson Software**, renamed to **Softlaw Corporation** in 1983 (per *Tandy's Little Wonder*). VIP Writer III source code rights later transferred to **SD Enterprises**.
- **Telewriter author** — earlier drafts left blank pending research. Now attributed to **Howard Cohen** (Cognitec) per Coco Chronicles.
- **Color BASIC authorship** — earlier drafts pointed at an unspecified "Microsoft team." Wikipedia cites **Bill Gates** explicitly as Color BASIC's designer, and quotes Gates stating he personally wrote Extended Color BASIC. Note: this likely simplifies the contributions of a supporting Microsoft team whose names remain undocumented.
- **GIME designers** — the CoCo 3's custom ASIC designers (**J.M. Prickett** / Tandy and **J.L. Bruister** / VLSI Technology) are documented in Pitre & Loguidice (2013) and visible on the chip's printed circuitry.
- **Boisy Pitre biography** — confirmed he joined Microware as a software engineer in 1992; co-authored the 2013 history book with Bill Loguidice.
- **Simply Better spelling** — some community sources spell "Marentes" as "Marantes" (including the Sub-Etha article); the canonical spelling is **Marentes**.
- **Steve Bjork double-credit** — expanded Bjork's entry beyond games-only. He was also a contracted CoCo 3 developer and advocated for hardware features (enhanced IRQ timer), crossing the usual line between commercial game developers and first-party engineers.
- **Tony DiStefano double-credit** — previously only a hardware figure. He also served as a *Rainbow Magazine* Contributing Editor on the 1985 masthead, so Disto hardware development and Rainbow writing ran in parallel.
- **KISSable OS-9 premiere date** — now confirmed as **October 1983** (previously undated). Puckett's Basic09 Tour Guide authorship also added.
- **Rainbow tenure** — confirmed as **July 1981 – May 1993** (previously "1981–1993").
- **Lonnie Falk name** — full name "Lawrence C. Falk" added; he went by "Lonnie" personally. Also confirmed his editorial column title was "PRINT#-2," (with intentional trailing comma as CoCo BASIC syntax).
- **William Astle, NOT "William Asher"** — **significant correction.** Earlier drafts (including the initial `companies.md` and `whos-who.md` under issue #416) spelled the lwtools author's name "William Asher." Correct spelling is **William Astle** (of Lost Wizard Enterprises, Canada, lost.l-w.ca). Both documents have been corrected and the entry now lives under "Astle" in whos-who.md.
- **NitrOS-9 origin — Boisy Pitre is NOT the original lead.** Earlier drafts described Pitre as "NitrOS-9 lead developer." Research now establishes the project was originated by **Bill Noble, Wes Gayle, and L. Curtis Boyle** (all Canadians). Pitre is a major contributor from v3.2.8 onward — important, but distinct from "creator." His entry has been rewritten.
- **Microware co-founders — Ken Kaplan + Larry Crane, NOT Ken Kaplan + Terry Ritter.** Terry Ritter was at Motorola (6809 designer, *Byte* article author) and his ideas influenced OS-9's architecture, but he was not a Microware principal. This is a commonly repeated error in community sources — corrected here with explicit note on Ritter's entry.
- **Microware founding date and location** — 1977, Clive, Iowa (confirmed via Wikipedia). Earlier drafts unspecified.

## Still Needed

Gaps in the citation chain that future research should close:

- Individual programmer(s) behind VIP Writer / VIP Calc at Softlaw (company founder is "Nelson" per *Tandy's Little Wonder*, but the specific programmer is unverified).
- PXE Computing's principal author (AutoTerm).
- Robert Kilgus's biographical details beyond authorship of Color SCRIPSIT.
- Microsoft engineers (besides Bill Gates) who contributed to Color BASIC / Extended Color BASIC. Gates is the only named designer in public sources, but the actual development likely involved a team.
- Tandy's CoCo 1 and CoCo 2 hardware engineering leads. CoCo 3 has **Mark Siegel** (champion), **J.M. Prickett** (GIME chip co-designer), and **Steve Bjork** (contracted developer) publicly documented, but the equivalent names for the 1980 and 1983 machines remain unknown.
- Microware engineers specifically credited on the Super ECB ROM extensions for the CoCo 3 (company confirmed; individuals not yet named).
- J&M Systems, Ltd. founder and principals — Alan Stallings documented as "formerly of J&M" but not confirmed as founder.
- CoCoTALK! / The CoCo Nation Show primary host name — the site does not explicitly credit a host, only naming L. Curtis Boyle as a regular contributor.
- The CoCo Crew Podcast "Neil" (full name), and the exact role of Mike in the recurring lineup.
- Dale Rickert's other software (if any) beyond *Simply Better*.
- Full credits for *Color SCRIPSIT II* (1987) — stated to be a completely new program, not a Kilgus revision.
