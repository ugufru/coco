# CoCo Who's Who

Alphabetical roster of identified individuals from the TRS-80 Color Computer community — classic era through today. Each entry notes known affiliations and products with cross-references into [companies.md](companies.md), [README.md](README.md) (the Hall of Fame), and [references.md](references.md) (source citations).

This is a working research document. Uncertain claims are marked **(verify)** and will be replaced with primary-source citations as research continues.

---

## A

**Rick Adams** — Co-creator of *Dungeons of Daggorath* with Phil DeLong. The real-time 3D dungeon crawler is widely considered one of the greatest CoCo games ever made.

**Scott Adams** — Founder of **Adventure International** (1979, Longwood, Florida) and widely credited as the **father of the microcomputer adventure game**. Wrote *Adventureland* in 1978 — the first text adventure to run on a home microcomputer (originally TRS-80 Model I, 16K). The *Scott Adams Adventures* series (*Adventureland*, *Pirate Adventure*, *Mission Impossible*, *Voodoo Castle*, *The Count*, *Strange Odyssey*, *Mystery Fun House*, *Pyramid of Doom*, *Ghost Town*, *Savage Island* I & II, *Golden Voyage*, *Sorcerer of Claymorgue Castle*) was ported to the TRS-80 Color Computer and remained a defining adventure-game catalog on the platform. Later collaborated with Marvel on the **Questprobe** series (*The Hulk*, *Spider-Man*). **(Not to be confused with Scott Adams the *Dilbert* cartoonist — different person, same name.)** **(verify exact CoCo release years for each title)**

**Bob Albrecht** — *Rainbow Magazine* Contributing Editor (1985 masthead).

**Cray Augsburg** — *Rainbow Magazine* Technical Editor (1987), promoted to Managing Editor by August 1992 — carried the magazine's top editorial role through its final years leading up to the May 1993 close.

**Ciaran Anscomb** — Author of *XRoar*, the primary CoCo/Dragon emulator used for cross-development. Accurate, actively maintained, essential to the modern development workflow.

**Robert Arnstein** — Tandy/Radio Shack in-house programmer. Wrote many of the official CoCo cassette software titles that shipped through Radio Shack stores.

**William Astle** — Lost Wizard Enterprises (Canada). Creator of *lwtools* (lwasm / lwlink) — the modern 6809/6309 cross-assembler and linker toolchain; de facto standard for contemporary CoCo development. lwasm is the assembler used to build the NitrOS-9 project itself. Also a modern NitrOS-9 contributor (DriveWire support, SuperDriver, driver fixes). Site: lost.l-w.ca. (**Correction:** earlier drafts of this roster incorrectly spelled his name "Asher" — the correct spelling is **Astle**.)

## B

**William Barden Jr.** — Author of foundational CoCo programming books including *Color Computer Assembly Language Programming*. Taught a generation how the 6809 worked.

**Jim Brain** — President of the Glenside Color Computer Club (current). Also a wider retrocomputing figure — founder of **Retro Innovations**, producing hardware for multiple vintage platforms. His leadership continues Glenside's hosting of the annual Chicago CoCoFEST!.

**Steve Bjork** — Spectral Associates. *Zaxxon*, *Astro Blast*, and numerous Tandy-licensed titles — one of the most prolific CoCo game developers of the 1980s. Also one of the original Tandy CoCo 3 developers (contract work), where he advocated for the enhanced IRQ timer functionality that later shaped the CoCo 3's capabilities. A rare figure whose work spans both commercial games and first-party hardware development.

**J.L. Bruister** — VLSI Technology. Co-designer of the GIME ASIC (Graphics Interrupt Memory Enhancement) chip for the CoCo 3 alongside J.M. Prickett. Name visible on the chip's circuitry.

**R. Bartly Betts** — *Rainbow Magazine* Contributing Editor (1985 masthead).

**Neil Blanchard** — CoCo community contributor. **(verify specifics)**

**Steve Blyn** — *Rainbow Magazine* Contributing Editor (1985 masthead); associated with Computer Island and CoCo educational software.

**L. Curtis Boyle** — One of the **three original co-creators of NitrOS-9** (with Bill Noble and Wes Gayle — all three Canadians) — the modernized OS-9 for CoCo 3. Also leads **NitrOS-9 Ease of Use (EOU)** — the user-facing distribution that serves as proving ground for changes before they reach the official NitrOS-9 repository. Maintains the definitive online catalog of commercial CoCo games at lcurtisboyle.com/nitros9, with developer interviews, screenshots, and permissions-cleared downloads. Preserved the rare 1986 Radio Shack CoCo 3 demo; conducted the Ken Kalish interview documenting Spectral Associates history; authored the OS9 DSK Converter utility. Also a regular contributor to CoCoTALK! / The CoCo Nation Show with recurring news segments.

**Chris Burke** — Burke & Burke. Principal behind the **CoCo XT Hard Disk Interface** (v2.4 1989, v2.5 1990) and **CoCo XT-RTC** (with real-time clock) — the primary way CoCo 3 users attached PC-style hard drives via standard MFM/RLL controllers. Also associated with **PowerBoost** (verification pending) and various OS-9 utilities. Major figure in the CoCo 3 OS-9 hardware era.

## C

**Wayne Campbell** — OS-9 utilities and ports; prolific OS-9 application author.

**Larry Crane** — Co-founder of **Microware Systems Corporation** (with Ken Kaplan), 1977, Clive, Iowa. Helped design OS-9's file managers and general internals.

**Howard Cohen** — Cognitec. Author of *Telewriter* (November 1983), *Telewriter-64*, and *Telewriter-128* — the CoCo's dominant pre-OS-9 word processor and the first widely regarded as a "world-class" product on the platform.

**H. Allen Curtis** — Long-running *Rainbow Magazine* contributor (Nov 1982 through at least April 1988), specializing in CoCo graphics, color/palette techniques, and reference utilities. Confirmed articles include *Shades of PMODE3* (Nov 1982), *MC-10 Memory Map* (Jan 1984 — the MC-10 follow-up to Bob Russell's CoCo memory map series, pitched as "Reading little brother's mind"), *Little E* editor (April 1984), *A Feline CoCo 3 Fandango* (May 1987 graphics tutorial, p. 52, cross-promoted by the editor from the Letters page), and *PALETTEable Color Mixing* (April 1988, p. 124). No biographical details (hometown, profession, employer) surface in available sources. (**Correction:** earlier drafts of this roster spelled the name "Curtiss" with two s's — every Rainbow byline I've located uses "Curtis." **Earlier drafts also credited him with desktop publishing software — that claim is unverified and likely a misattribution; flagged for removal pending evidence.**)

## D

**Glen Dahlgren** — Involved with *Dungeons of Daggorath* preservation and revival efforts.

**Kevin Darling** — Delphi CoCo SIG sysop; prolific OS-9 technical author through the 1980s and 90s.

**Robert Doggett** — Early **Microware Systems Corporation** engineer (with Ken Kaplan and Larry Crane). Helped develop OS-9's file managers and general internals.

**Phil DeLong** — Co-creator of *Dungeons of Daggorath* with Rick Adams.

**Mark Davidsaver** — Eigen Systems. Author of *Colorcom/E* terminal program, a popular CoCo comms utility with cassette file transfer, data capture, and RS-232 configuration.

**R. Wayne Day** — *Rainbow Magazine* Contributing Editor (1985 masthead).

**Peter Dibble** — OS-9 author and columnist. Wrote a monthly OS-9 column for *68 Micro Journal*. Co-authored (with Dale Puckett) *The Complete Rainbow Guide to OS-9* (Falsoft, 1985). Served as Vice President of the OS-9 Users Group (Puckett was President).

**Bob Devries** — Australian contributor. OS-9 community work, DriveWire contributions.

**Tony Di Stefano** — CRC Computers Inc. / Disto Super Products, Montreal, Canada. Founded 1983. Designed and marketed the *Disto* product line: *Super Disk Controller* and *Super Disk Controller II* (the latter sold at Radio Shack), *Mini Controller*, *DISPLAY80* 80-column card, *Mini EPROM Programmer*, *Hard Disk II Adapter*, *Super RAMDisk*, *Super RAM 3* (512K with OS-9 Level 2 utilities), *MEB Adapter*, and *RS-232 SuperPack*. One of the largest third-party CoCo hardware vendors of the 1980s. (Also spelled "DiStefano" in older sources.) Also served as a *Rainbow Magazine* Contributing Editor on the 1985 masthead — the Disto hardware line and Rainbow writing ran in parallel.

**Dan Downard** — *Rainbow Magazine* Technical Editor (1985).

## E

**Chuck Ekblad** — Game developer; multiple CoCo titles.

## F

**Lawrence C. "Lonnie" Falk** — Editor and Publisher of *The Rainbow Magazine* (July 1981 – May 1993), the CoCo's essential monthly periodical. Founder of Falsoft, Inc. (Prospect, Kentucky). Wrote the magazine's signature monthly editorial column, **"PRINT#-2,"** — the trailing comma is deliberate CoCo BASIC syntax (print-to-printer). More than a publisher: Falk was the community's connective tissue through the platform's commercial era.

**Art Flexser** — Spectro Systems. Author of *ADOS*, *ADOS 3*, and *Extended ADOS 3* — arguably the most popular modified RS-DOS for the CoCo, adding 40/80-track drive support, double-speed disk I/O, and command-line editing.

## G

**Wes Gayle** — One of the three original co-creators of **NitrOS-9** (with Bill Noble and L. Curtis Boyle — all Canadians). Started the project as an enhanced OS-9 Level II for the CoCo 3.

**Bill Gates** — Microsoft co-founder. Publicly credited as the designer of Color BASIC for the TRS-80 Color Computer, and stated in period interviews that he wrote Extended Color BASIC himself. Microsoft's Color BASIC ROM shipped in every CoCo from 1980 through 1991, which makes Gates — somewhat surprisingly — one of the most-installed software authors on the platform.

**Robert Gault** — Longtime *Rainbow Magazine* technical contributor; OS-9 patches and utilities. Modern-era **NitrOS-9** contributor, particularly on hardware ports and driver fixes.

**Jim Gerrie** — MC-10 and CoCo BASIC game ports and originals. Prolific modern-era contributor keeping BASIC-era game development alive.

**Stephen Goldberg** — Hyper-I/O. *HyperDriver* hard drive system for OS-9 on CoCo. **(verify)**

**Marty Goodman** — Deep technical articles on CoCo hardware internals. Go-to source for signal-level understanding of the machine.

**Brett Gordon** — Modern toolchain work; GCC 6809 contributions. **(verify specifics)**

## H

**Jim Hathaway** — DriveWire contributor. **(verify)**

**Christopher R. (Chris) Hawks** — Hawksoft, Harvard, Illinois. Long-running CoCo hardware and software vendor. Products include *CDF OS9* (NitrOS-9 CD-ROM driver), *RGB-to-S-Video Converter*, and the *Raspberry Pi CoCo* lookalike. Continues to produce CoCo-related products in the modern era.

A key figure in the post-CoCo-3 OS-9 hardware scene.

**Allen Huffman** — Sub-Etha Software (OS-9 era). CoCo blogger and community historian; extensive writing on CoCo history and personal development experiences.

## K

**Robert Kilgus** — Tandy. Author of *Color SCRIPSIT* (1981, cat. 26-3105) — the first-party CoCo word processor that shipped as a Program Pak cartridge, with a disk version following in 1982 (26-3255) adding lowercase display, underline, and printer control codes.

**Joseph Kolar** — *Rainbow Magazine* Contributing Editor (1985 masthead).

**John Kowalski (SockMaster)** — Demo scene legend. Pushed CoCo graphics far beyond what the hardware was thought capable of. His demos remain jaw-dropping technical achievements.

**David Ladd** — Modern-era **NitrOS-9** contributor. Major work since v3.2.8, including DriveWire support, the SuperDriver, hardware ports, and driver fixes.

## P

**Kevin Pease** — OS-9 utilities. **(verify specifics)**

**Michael Plog** — *Rainbow Magazine* Contributing Editor (1985 masthead).

**George Phillips** — CoCo community contributor. Conducted the 2015 interview with Joshua Lavinsky preserving important early-TRS-80 software history.

**Boisy G. Pitre** — Major modern-era contributor to **NitrOS-9** (a critical figure since v3.2.8 — NOT the original creator; NitrOS-9 was originated by Bill Noble, Wes Gayle, and L. Curtis Boyle). **J.M. Prickett** — Tandy engineer. Co-designer of the GIME ASIC for the CoCo 3 alongside J.L. Bruister of VLSI Technology. Name visible on the chip's circuitry — the only Tandy hardware engineer named in publicly documented chip credits for the CoCo 3.

Co-authored (with Peter Dibble) *The Complete Rainbow Guide to OS-9* (Falsoft, 1985), 400+ pages plus two accompanying disks. Also wrote *Microware's Official Basic09 Tour Guide*. Elected President of the OS-9 Users Group (Peter Dibble served as VP).

## R

**James E. "Jim" Reed** — *Rainbow Magazine* Managing Editor (confirmed 1985–1987), later promoted to Assistant Vice President of Programs & Projects at Falsoft, Inc.

**Bob Russell** — R. R. Enterprises. Author of *Color Computer Memory Map* — a hand-researched reference cataloging CoCo system variables, BASIC internals, ROM entry points, and hardware registers that Radio Shack never officially documented. The work was delivered on two channels in parallel during 1983: as a multi-part series in *The Rainbow Magazine* billed each month as "CoCo's most complete memory map" (**Part I** July 1983, **Part II** August 1983, **Part III** September 1983, **Part IV** December 1983), and as a privately-published consolidated edition (Version 2.0, 10/01/83) distributed through R. R. Enterprises mid-series. By his own account in the introduction, the project consumed 650+ hours of reverse-engineering and cross-referencing against the TRS-80 Color Computer Service Manual, *Color Computer News*, *The Rainbow*, *80 Micro*, and *Hot CoCo* — undertaken because Tandy's published references (Getting Started With Color Basic, Going Ahead With Extended Basic, TRS-80 Microcomputer News) were "frequently incorrect." Planned annual updates and offered a 40% discount to subscribers who reported errors and omissions — an early example of the community-maintained living-document model. Russell's series directly seeded H. Allen Curtis's MC-10 memory map (Rainbow, January 1984). The PDF remains preserved on the Color Computer Archive and os9projects.com, where it continues to serve as a primary reference for modern CoCo development. **(no further biographical details — hometown, other products, or Rainbow masthead role — surface in available sources)**

**Dale Rickert** — Author of *Simply Better*, a CoCo 3 word processor widely admired for its feature depth — compact executable, substantial (~137-page) manual.

**Terry Ritter** — Motorola engineer. Designer of the **6809 CPU** and author of a foundational three-part *Byte* article series (Jan–Mar 1979) on OS-9's modular design — which influenced Microware's architecture. **Correction note:** Ritter is often incorrectly identified as a Microware co-founder in community sources; he was at Motorola, not Microware. The actual Microware co-founders were Ken Kaplan and Larry Crane.

**Mike Rowen** — CoCo Crew Podcast contributor (mike@cococrew.org). **(verify full role)**

## S

**Fred Scerbo** — *Rainbow Magazine* Contributing Editor (1985 masthead).

**Pierre Sarrazin** — Creator of *CMOC*, a working C compiler targeting 6809/CoCo. A major modern cross-development tool that opened C programming to the platform.

**Alan Stallings** — formerly of J&M Systems, Ltd. (New Mexico). Publicly documented J&M associate; credited in CoCopedia for correcting and expanding the J&M controller record. J&M produced the *JFD-COCO*, *JFD-CP*, and *JFD-EC* disk controllers with JDOS 1.11 ROM. Actual J&M founder's name remains unconfirmed.

**Mark Siegel** — Tandy. Widely regarded as the "father of the CoCo 3" and the CoCo's biggest internal supporter at Tandy. Championed the product through its final Radio Shack generation (1986) and later provided first-hand accounts of the prototype and development history to CoCo historians.

**Ed Snider** — Creator of the *CoCoSDC*, an SD card interface for real CoCo hardware. Brought modern storage to vintage machines.

**John Strong** — Strongware. CoCo game developer and development-tool author. Frequent featured guest on CoCoTALK! / The CoCo Nation Show, where he has demonstrated CoCo projects and game-creation tools. A connecting figure between the modern-era game developer community and the livestream/podcast scene.

**Bob Swoger** — Communications Director, Glenside Color Computer Club. Longtime partner with Tony Podraza in coordinating CoCoFEST! logistics and vendor materials.

**Frank G. Swygert** — FARNA Systems. Author of *Tandy's Little Wonder: The Color Computer 1979-1991* (1993), the canonical printed reference on CoCo history, hardware, and software — a key primary source for this Hall of Fame.

## T

**Roger Taylor** — Hardware projects and community contributions spanning multiple CoCo generations. Launched **Project 256**, a community effort to reverse engineer the CoCo 3 GIME chip and investigate rumored-but-unconfirmed graphics modes.

Disk BASIC titles include *Ultra Editor*, *Our DOS*, *Disk Utility Pack II*, *HiRes+*, and *Magazine Index System*. Continues modern software work at mellowood.ca.
