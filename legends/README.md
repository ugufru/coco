# CoCo Hall of Fame

Notable software engineers, hardware designers, authors, and community figures from the TRS-80 Color Computer world — classic era through today.

This document is curated and narrative: it highlights the genres and subgroups that defined the CoCo ecosystem, calls out the relationships between people and projects, and preserves the references, milestones, and quiet contributions that made the platform what it was.

It is a working document. Entries are starting points meant to be expanded with deeper research. Details are compiled from community knowledge and should be fact-checked against primary sources (Rainbow Magazine archives, CoCo mailing list history, personal sites, CoCoFEST presentations).

## Genres & Subgroups

The CoCo world was never one community — it was several overlapping ones, each with its own gravity. What follows is a tour of those subgroups, with the people most associated with each. For full rosters, see [whos-who.md](whos-who.md).

### Game Developers

The biggest public face of the platform. Radio Shack sold CoCos to families, and families bought games. The commercial game era ran from about 1980 through the late 1980s, with a remarkable modern revival continuing today.

Key names: **Steve Bjork**, **Ken Kalish**, **Steve Ostrom** (Spectral Associates); **Rick Adams** and **Phil DeLong** (*Dungeons of Daggorath*); **Nick Marentes** (modern era, Australia); **Jim Gerrie** (BASIC ports); **Robert Arnstein** (Tandy in-house); **Greg Zumwalt**, **Chuck Ekblad**.

### Demo Scene

A small but technically extraordinary subgroup. Demos prove what the hardware can *really* do when nobody is worrying about shipping on time.

Key names: **John Kowalski (SockMaster)** — the headline name; demos that redefined the CoCo 3's graphical ceiling.

### OS & Systems Software

Microware's OS-9 turned the CoCo into a serious multitasking machine, and the community has kept that lineage alive for forty years. This is where the CoCo's post-commercial life has been richest.

Key names (original era): **Ken Kaplan** + **Larry Crane** (Microware co-founders, 1977), **Robert Doggett** (early Microware engineer). Key names (NitrOS-9 originals): **Bill Noble**, **Wes Gayle**, **L. Curtis Boyle** — three Canadians who began NitrOS-9 as an enhanced OS-9 Level II for the CoCo 3. Key names (modern NitrOS-9 continuation): **Boisy Pitre**, **David Ladd**, **William Astle**, **Robert Gault**, **Aaron Wolfe**. Adjacent OS-9 community authors: **Wayne Campbell**, **Kevin Darling**, **Dale Puckett**, **Peter Dibble**, **Allen Huffman**.

### Tools, Compilers & Emulators

Modern cross-development is what makes CoCo work feasible in 2026 — and it all rests on a handful of tools maintained by a handful of people.

Key names: **William Astle** (lwtools — the assembler; Lost Wizard Enterprises), **Pierre Sarrazin** (CMOC — the C compiler), **Ciaran Anscomb** (XRoar — the emulator), **Tim Lindner** (MAME driver accuracy), **Jeff Vavasour** (the original CoCo emulator), **Nathan Woods** (original MAME CoCo driver).

### Hardware Designers

Third-party hardware has always been how CoCo owners transcended their stock machines. From 1980s disk controllers to modern SD interfaces. First-party hardware credits belong to a small group of Tandy and Motorola Semiconductor engineers.

Key names (Tandy / first-party): **Mark Siegel** (CoCo 3 internal champion), **J.M. Prickett** (Tandy GIME co-designer), **J.L. Bruister** (VLSI Technology GIME co-designer), **Steve Bjork** (contracted CoCo 3 developer, IRQ timer advocate).

Key names (third-party): **Tony DiStefano** (Disto), **Mark Marlette** (Cloud-9 Tech), **Frank Hogg** (FHL), **Chris Hawks** (Hawksoft), **Ed Snider** (CoCoSDC), **Roger Taylor** (Project 256 GIME reverse engineering).

### Applications & Productivity Software

The quieter half of CoCo commercial software: word processors, terminal programs, spreadsheets, databases, modified DOSes. The tools people actually used day to day.

Key names: **Howard Cohen** (Cognitec — *Telewriter*, first "world-class" CoCo word processor); **Robert Kilgus** (Tandy — *Color SCRIPSIT*); **Art Flexser** (Spectro Systems — *ADOS-3*); **Mark Davidsaver** (Eigen Systems — *Colorcom/E*); **Dale Rickert** (*Simply Better*); **Bob van der Poel** (*Ved*, *Vprint*, *Ultra Editor*, and a 29-title catalog); Softlaw Corporation (formerly Nelson Software) — *VIP Writer/Calc/Library/Terminal/Database* suite; PXE Computing (*AutoTerm*).

### Books, Magazines & Education

The CoCo community was built on paper long before it was built on the internet. *The Rainbow* magazine and a handful of foundational books taught hundreds of thousands of people how to program.

Key names: **William Barden Jr.** (foundational assembly books), **Lonnie Falk** (publisher of *The Rainbow*; "PRINT#-2," editorial column), **Jim Reed** (Rainbow Managing Editor), **Cray Augsburg** (Technical → Managing Editor), **Dale Puckett** (KISSable OS-9 column; *Complete Rainbow Guide to OS-9* with Peter Dibble), **Peter Dibble** (OS-9 author; VP OS-9 Users Group), **Marty Goodman** (hardware-internals articles). Rainbow's 1985 contributing editors were themselves a hall-of-fame slice of the community — including **Tony DiStefano** (who wrote for Rainbow while building Disto hardware).

### Community & Preservation

The people who kept the lights on — running fests, maintaining archives, recording the oral history, preserving rare software.

Key names (classic era → modern): **Tony Podraza** (Glenside CCC past President, CoCoFEST Coordinator Emeritus), **Jim Brain** (current Glenside President, Retro Innovations founder), **John Mark Mobley** (Glenside VP), **Grant Leighty** (CoCoFEST Coordinator), **Ian Mavric**, **Bob Swoger** (Communications); **John W. Linville** (CoCoFEST organizer, CoCo Crew Podcast host, FMV programmer); **L. Curtis Boyle** (archives, interviews, CoCoTALK! news segments); **George Phillips** (historical interviews); **Glen Dahlgren** (Daggorath preservation); **John Strong** (Strongware, CoCoTALK! regular).

## Milestones

Specific moments and artifacts that define the arc of the platform.

- **1977** — Joint Tandy / Motorola Semiconductor venture begins on a videotex terminal for the Kentucky Cooperative Extension Service. This project seeds the hardware that will become the Color Computer.
- **1980** — CoCo 1 ships with **Bill Gates**-credited Color BASIC 1.0 (Microsoft) in ROM — the only programming environment out of the box.
- **1982** — *Dungeons of Daggorath* released. Real-time 3D dungeon crawler on a 64K home computer — years ahead of its time.
- **1981** — **Robert Kilgus**'s *Color SCRIPSIT* (cat. 26-3105) ships as a Tandy cartridge — the first-party CoCo word processor.
- **July 1981 – May 1993** — *The Rainbow Magazine* publishes monthly from Falsoft in Prospect, Kentucky; Lonnie Falk's **"PRINT#-2,"** editorial column runs throughout. The connective tissue of the CoCo community.
- **October 1983** — **Dale Puckett's** *KISSable OS-9* column debuts in *The Rainbow* — the platform's definitive recurring OS-9 column for the next decade.
- **1985** — **Dale Puckett** and **Peter Dibble** publish *The Complete Rainbow Guide to OS-9* through Falsoft — 400+ pages plus two disks; the CoCo OS-9 reference.
- **November 1983** — **Howard Cohen**'s *Telewriter* (Cognitec) releases and becomes the first "world-class" CoCo word processor; Telewriter-64 and Telewriter-128 follow.
- **1983** — CoCo 2 ships. OS-9 Level I available. Nelson Software renames to **Softlaw Corporation** and launches the *VIP* productivity suite.
- **1986** — CoCo 3 ships. Championed internally at Tandy by **Mark Siegel** ("father of the CoCo 3"); GIME custom ASIC co-designed by **J.M. Prickett** (Tandy) and **J.L. Bruister** (VLSI Technology); **Steve Bjork** contracted as one of the original CoCo 3 developers. **Microware** writes the Super Extended Color BASIC ROM extensions. OS-9 Level II turns it into a proper multitasking machine.
- **1980s demo scene** — **John Kowalski (SockMaster)** demos redefine what CoCo 3 graphics can do.
- **Late 1980s–90s** — Third-party hardware boom: Disto, FHL, J&M, Burke & Burke expansions push the platform past its design ceiling.
- **1990s** — Jeff Vavasour's Color Computer Emulator makes the CoCo portable to PCs for the first time.
- **1977** — **Microware Systems Corporation** founded in Clive, Iowa by **Ken Kaplan** and **Larry Crane** under an NSF grant for 6800 software. The company's shift to the 6809 (1978) produces **BASIC09** and then **OS-9** — the OS that will come to define the CoCo's second life.
- **January–March 1979** — **Terry Ritter** (Motorola, 6809 designer) publishes a three-part *Byte* article series on OS-9's modular design — a foundational document that shapes the OS's architecture.
- **1990s** — Three Canadians — **Bill Noble**, **Wes Gayle**, and **L. Curtis Boyle** — begin **NitrOS-9** as an enhanced OS-9 Level II for the CoCo 3. The project modernizes OS-9 and gives the platform a maintained OS for the modern era.
- **September 2001** — RadiSys Corp. acquires Microware Systems. Ken Kaplan retires in 2002 after 25 years.
- **March 1, 2013** — A distributor group purchases OS-9 rights, forming **Microware LP** — keeping commercial OS-9 alive alongside the community NitrOS-9 line.
- **2013** — **Boisy Pitre** and **Bill Loguidice** publish *CoCo: The Colorful History of Tandy's Underdog Computer* (CRC Press) — the first full-length history book of the platform, with first-hand accounts from Tandy executives and engineers.
- **Mid-2010s–ongoing** — **The CoCo Crew Podcast** (John Linville, Neil, and lineup) and **CoCoTALK! / The CoCo Nation Show** (weekly live, L. Curtis Boyle news segments, 465+ episodes) establish the modern podcast/livestream pillars of the community.
- **April 24–25, 2026** — The 34th Annual "Last" Chicago **CoCoFEST!** — running under Jim Brain (President) and Grant Leighty (Coordinator) with the Glenside Color Computer Club. One of the longest-running single-platform retrocomputing gatherings anywhere.
- **2010s** — **CoCoSDC** (Ed Snider) brings practical SD-card storage to real hardware.
- **2015** — George Phillips' interview with Joshua Lavinsky preserves the history of *Space Warp* / *Time Trek* — a pivotal piece of 4K TRS-80 software history.
- **Present** — Active modern development: new games (Marentes, Gerrie), new hardware (Cloud-9), new tools (lwtools, CMOC, XRoar), annual CoCoFEST.

## Relationships

The threads that connect people across the ecosystem.

- **Spectral Associates lineage** — Ken Kalish, Steve Bjork, Steve Ostrom. One of the era's defining game houses; catalog and history preserved through L. Curtis Boyle's Kalish interview.
- **Dungeons of Daggorath circle** — Rick Adams and Phil DeLong (creators), Glen Dahlgren (preservation/revival).
- **NitrOS-9 continuum** — Microware's OS-9 (Ken Kaplan + Larry Crane + Robert Doggett, 1977–2001) → the three Canadian originators of NitrOS-9 (Bill Noble + Wes Gayle + L. Curtis Boyle) → modern contributor wave (Boisy Pitre, David Ladd, William Astle, Robert Gault, Aaron Wolfe) → NitrOS-9 EOU distribution (L. Curtis Boyle). Boisy Pitre is uniquely positioned as a modern NitrOS-9 contributor who was also a Microware software engineer from 1992 — a direct human bridge between the commercial and community eras.
- **Modern cross-dev stack** — XRoar (Anscomb) + lwtools (Astle) + CMOC (Sarrazin) is the triad everyone uses today.
- **Emulator lineage** — Jeff Vavasour's early emulator → Nathan Woods' MAME driver → Tim Lindner's MAME accuracy work → Ciaran Anscomb's XRoar (now dominant).
- **Rainbow ecosystem** — Lonnie Falk (Editor/Publisher) led a masthead that evolved over the decade: Jim Reed as Managing Editor through the mid-80s (later promoted inside Falsoft); Cray Augsburg succeeding him through the magazine's final years; Dan Downard and later Cray Augsburg as Technical Editors; Dale Puckett's KISSable OS-9 column from October 1983; Marty Goodman on hardware internals; and a rotating Contributing Editor roster (Bob Albrecht, R. Wayne Day, Dennis Kitsz, Joseph Kolar, Michael Plog, Fred Scerbo, Steve Blyn, R. Bartly Betts, Richard White, and the Disto founder **Tony DiStefano**).
- **OS-9 Users Group** — Dale Puckett (President) / Peter Dibble (VP). Their Rainbow column and book partnership formalized the CoCo OS-9 scene.
- **Productivity author constellation** — Howard Cohen (Telewriter / Cognitec), Robert Kilgus (Color SCRIPSIT / Tandy), Art Flexser (ADOS / Spectro Systems), Mark Davidsaver (Colorcom/E / Eigen Systems), Dale Rickert (Simply Better), and Bob van der Poel (Ved, Vprint, ~29-title catalog) defined the CoCo's day-to-day software experience. The VIP line from Softlaw Corp (formerly Nelson Software) completed the suite.
- **CoCo 3 origin circle** — Mark Siegel (Tandy internal champion) / J.M. Prickett (Tandy hardware) / J.L. Bruister (VLSI Technology) / Steve Bjork (contracted developer) / Microware (Super ECB ROM) together produced the CoCo 3 in 1986. This crosses the usual line between "commercial game developers" and "first-party hardware engineers" — Bjork belongs to both sides.
- **Microsoft → Microware handoff** — Bill Gates and Microsoft authored the Color BASIC / Extended Color BASIC / Disk Extended Color BASIC ROMs across CoCo 1/2; when the CoCo 3 arrived in 1986, Microware (not Microsoft) wrote the Super ECB extensions on top.
- **OS-9 hardware vendors** — Burke & Burke, Frank Hogg Labs, and Disto were the names you saw on every OS-9 ad in Rainbow.
- **Community preservation thread** — L. Curtis Boyle's catalog + George Phillips' interviews + Allen Huffman's blog writing + the CoCo Crew Podcast form a living archive.
- **Glenside through-line** — Tony Podraza (past President, CoCoFEST Coordinator Emeritus) → Jim Brain (current President) + Grant Leighty (Coordinator) + Bob Swoger (Communications) + Ian Mavric + John Mark Mobley. The club is the reason there's still a CoCoFEST in 2026.
- **Modern media pillars** — *CoCo Crew Podcast* (John Linville + Neil + extended lineup including Boisy Pitre) and *CoCoTALK!* / *The CoCo Nation Show* (weekly live, L. Curtis Boyle as recurring contributor, John Strong as regular guest) serve as the audio/video counterparts to the text-heavy Rainbow era.

## References & Primary Sources

Where to go for verified history and artifacts.

- **[lcurtisboyle.com/nitros9](https://lcurtisboyle.com/nitros9)** — Definitive online catalog of commercial CoCo games with developer notes, screenshots, easter eggs, and permissions-cleared downloads. Also hosts the L. Curtis Boyle / Ken Kalish interview documenting Spectral Associates history and the preserved 1986 Radio Shack CoCo 3 demo disk.
- **Frank G. Swygert, *Tandy's Little Wonder: The Color Computer 1979-1991*** (FARNA Systems, 1993) — canonical printed history of the platform; primary source for many of the authorship attributions in this Hall of Fame. Available on the Color Computer Archive and Internet Archive.
- **[colorcomputerarchive.com](https://colorcomputerarchive.com)** — Comprehensive archive of CoCo manuals, software, magazines, and books. Primary source for productivity-title research (Telewriter, VIP Writer, Colorcom, ADOS, and more).
- **Coco Chronicles (bbsdocumentary.com)** — Community history with credits and release dates for many commercial CoCo titles; confirms Howard Cohen of Cognitec as the author of Telewriter.
- **George Phillips' 2015 Joshua Lavinsky interview** — Primary source on *Space Warp* / *Time Trek* and the 4K-assembly era of TRS-80 software.
- **The Rainbow Magazine archives** — Scanned monthly issues (1981–1993) preserved by community efforts; the single most comprehensive paper record of the platform.
- **CoCoFEST presentations** — Annual talks that often capture first-person history from developers and hardware designers.
- **The CoCo mailing list archive** — Decades of technical discussion and community thread; primary source on the OS-9 scene's evolution.
- **Sub-Etha Software blog (Allen Huffman)** — Ongoing first-person writing from someone who was there.
